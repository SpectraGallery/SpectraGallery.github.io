A Surprising Dream

I met Olive and Dandi last summer in a youth hostel. I was invited to their exhibition. It was my first time visiting a studio-sized exhibition, and I was impressed with how well-organised it was. The exhibition was small but concise, and I immediately knew that I had come across two interesting souls.

My feeling toward this year's exhibition is quite different from that last one. Apart from the fact that I am getting to know them better, I have sensed a transformation in both Dandi and Olive. In fact, the title of the exhibition, A Surprising Dream - a scene from the Chinese opera "The Peony Pavilion "- clearly states the artists' mindset change. Such a self-reflective transformation probably endows them with a new consciousness of their former dreaming beliefs. Although I am very curious about how they could acquire a new perspective, I would concentrate my discussion on their respective artworks, mainly.

Olive's work has a feature of looking inward and expressing her thoughts, feelings, and probably some behaviours. The paintings, like her self-talks, are full of consistent patterns of behaviours and emotions that may be holding her back. However, this time, Olive seems to acknowledge and better understand these patterns; she has begun to challenge and change the repetitive structure, replacing negative self-talk with positive affirmations. Probably this is her way of taking steps to retract the dreaming beliefs. Olive's ceramic sculptures of hundred horses represent, I quoted, "a torrent of released, angry, suppressed emotion in my heart." This work of collective figures is no longer a self- dialogue. It is clear that Olive becomes more open to complexity and diversity. The artist attributed such a change to her exposure to different storytellers in a night bar. But I think it is also worth considering the collaborative and exploratory process in her creation as a result of this shift.

The external factors not only encouraged Olive to experiment with another art form, namely ceramic sculpture but also influenced the symbolism or imagery used in her art. She-wolf is a consistent topic in Olive's work. This time, Olive gave several different perspectives for interpreting this theme. The ceramic she-wolf emphasises maternal instinct, while the painting "Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon" hints at the hidden, mysterious, and uncertain character of the she-wolf. For me, both works deliver quite different tones than her first she- wolf painting in the series, where the classic composition, probably symbolising savage and cruelty, dominates. The emotions and mood conveyed through the artwork as symbols of the artist's internal struggles are different to me from the previous exhibition.

The negative symbols, like the screw in the angel's butt or the belt tying up the innocent and defenceless rabbit, are still there, but now they seem to appear at Olive's disposal in a light-hearted setting. The previous use of the autism angel may indicate Olive's personal connection to autism or a desire to express feelings of difference or otherness. In her current self-talks, I neither sense such isolation nor the difficulty of connection. This time I think Olive simply, by exposing herself to the audience, chose her familiar symbols to connect with Others, an act of portraying a depressive state to call for empathy on the feelings of helplessness and hopelessness.

Let's consider the other artist, Dandi. In her exhibition message, Dandi also labelled depression and anxiety as her mental, probably dreaming, state before arriving at Jingdezhen. Surprisingly, she was also deemed to be cured by the local storytellers. This magical force could be a useful psychoactive asset for recognising and disputing negative beliefs. It is a shame that I seem to be inaccessible to this force due to, probably, my refusal of alcohol and beverage.

Come back to the discussion. I was pondering over Dandi's choice of using twisted high heels in her work as a suggested desire to convey complex emotions that may be difficult to put into words. Dandi readily pointed me to consider high heels as a hermaphroditic metaphor, a transcend gender symbol that combines both masculine and feminine traits. For the moment, I recalled that heels were worn by both knights and ladies as a code, like Dandi said, of mixing toughness and grace. It is a genius idea to transcend gender identification with such a symbol. According to Dandi, twisting the symbol expresses an atypical development of sexuality and power. I think it can also represent a calling for a gender-neutral or gender-indifferent perspective on power and identity. The ceramic twisted high heels highlight the symbolic strength. They throw an open question to the visitors, letting them detach themselves from their usual identities and then project onto the desired realm.

Perhaps her painting is the easiest way to sense the change in Dandi's mindset. By switching from marker pens to brush, Dandi is experimenting with a new medium to draw her figures. Brushes provide more versatility and less control, allowing for a wider range of effects than markers. The alternation of the creative process represents that Dandi may stay with a more relaxed and confident mindset, breaking out of creative ruts or limiting controls. Through the brush strokes, one can observe the strength of the lines refreshing and energising. Giving up the controlled and precise tool, Dandi may embrace more unpredictable creation. The tendency only appears when the creator becomes more confident and has mastery of the creating subject. I hope the change will lead Dandi to new ways of thinking and creating.

Having mentioned the change, I am fully aware that Dandi still maintains simple composition and focuses on strong, bold shapes in her paintings. My guess is that this simplicity or minimalism could more or less relate to an inner calling for neutrality. Dandi's drawing figures are often gender-neutral; they are depicted with simplified, streamlined shapes that minimise gender-specific characteristics. Dandi may look for the use of simple, gender-neutral forms to create a sense of universality, emphasising the underlying essence or spirit of the subject rather than the outward appearance or identity. By focusing on simplicity and reduction, stripping away unnecessary details, Dandi is able to present the common ground for both gender-neutrality and minimalism. Perhaps, minimalism also helps Dandi detach her previous worries and anxieties, removing her mental burden in dreams.

To sum up, the exhibition provides a new image reflecting how the two artists explore their inner states during the trip to Jingdezhen. The artwork contains the artists' new inspiration and strength, recording their evolving creativity, a valuable memory generated between their dreaming and awakening states.

Both artists express some kind of compassion emergence during clay pinching or firing in the kiln. I am not sure how vital the developed compassion from the concern of fragility and instability is. But the whole process definitely makes two ladies awaken to their complex milieux. I wish the two ladies continue their powerful, creative processes after the complex enlightenment. And I am looking forward to visiting their next show.
